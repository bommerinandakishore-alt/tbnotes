Introduction & Definition

  • HIV-TB co-infection = presence of both HIV infection (active or latent) and tuberculosis (latent or active) in the same individual
  • Called the "Deadly Duet" — each disease accelerates the other's progression
  • TB is the most common opportunistic infection in HIV-infected individuals globally and in India
  • TB is the most common cause of death among AIDS patients, killing approximately 1 in 3
  • Pulmonary TB = WHO Clinical Stage 3; Extrapulmonary TB = WHO Clinical Stage 4
Key Dictum — Management Priority "Treat TB first, then ART" — initiate anti-TB treatment (ATT) immediately on TB diagnosis; start ART as early as possible within 2–8 weeks, depending on CD4 count

Bidirectional Pathogenesis

HIV → worsens TB
  • Kills CD4+ T-cells within granulomas
  • Impairs macrophage MTB killing
  • Reduces MTB-specific Th1 responses (IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-2)
  • Depletes BAL fluid CD4+ cells
  • Disrupts granuloma architecture
  • Increases reactivation + reinfection risk
  • Favours primary TB progression after exposure
⇄ BIDIRECTIONAL
TB → worsens HIV
  • Increases plasma HIV viremia (HIV RNA ↑)
  • Upregulates co-receptors CCR5 & CXCR4 → more HIV entry
  • Immune activation → more CD4 cells as HIV targets
  • Higher HIV loads in TB-involved lungs (BAL) and pleural fluid
  • Accelerates progression to AIDS and death
  • Viremia persists despite ATT (especially in Africa)
Net Result TB doubles HIV progression risk to AIDS/death. HIV doubles TB risk within the first year of seroconversion, and annual risk of active TB in HIV+ (5–10%) equals the lifetime risk in an HIV-negative individual.

🔺
HIV Viremia ↑
TB co-infection significantly raises plasma HIV RNA. Viremia declines after effective ATT but may persist at high levels in some African cohorts
🔑

MTB-induced increase in co-receptor expression on CD4+ cells → increased HIV entry, replication, and viraemia

Immune Activation
Generalised activation from TB increases proportion of CD4 cells that are preferential HIV targets → accelerated viral replication

TB is associated with increased risk of progression to AIDS and death. TB-associated deaths classified as HIV deaths (ICD-10)

TB not confirmed → Culture / LPA / Clinical judgment / Other OI workup
  • Sputum smear microscopy: Poor sensitivity in PLHIV (median 52.8%) due to fewer organisms + fewer cavities. AFB smear positive = highly specific for MTB in HIV even in MAC-endemic areas
  • CBNAAT / Xpert MTB/RIF: Preferred. Sensitivity 84%; also detects rifampicin resistance. Preferred for EPTB diagnosis too (CSF, pleural fluid, lymph node aspirate, urine)
  • Sputum culture (LJ / MGIT): Gold standard; sensitivity 85–100%; takes 2–8 weeks; essential for drug susceptibility testing (DST)
  • Line Probe Assay (LPA): Detects MDR-TB within 72 hours; used when resistance is suspected
  • Urine LAM (lipoarabinomannan): WHO-recommended for hospitalised PLHIV with CD4 ≤100 cells/µl or seriously ill patients. Sensitivity 56%; specificity 88–99%. Point-of-care (25 min, 60 µl urine)
  • Lymph node FNA: Positive in 77% of EPTB lymphadenopathy; biopsy + culture raises yield to 96%
  • Blood culture: Positive in 49% of disseminated TB with CD4 <100 cells/mm³
  • TST (Tuberculin Skin Test): Positivity defined at ≥5 mm induration in PLHIV. Frequently negative with low CD4. Not diagnostic for active TB in high-prevalence settings

Treatment — ATT & ART Timing

Golden Rule All HIV-TB co-infected patients should receive BOTH ATT and ART regardless of CD4 count. ATT is started first; ART follows based on CD4 timing to balance mortality reduction vs. IRIS risk.
CD4 Count ART Timing after starting ATT Rationale Key Trial
CD4 <50 cells/mm³ Within 2 weeks Highest mortality risk; early ART reduces AIDS-defining illness and death CAMELIA (NEJM 2011), SAPIT
CD4 50–200 cells/mm³ Within 2–8 weeks Balance between mortality reduction and IRIS risk STRIDE (ACTG 5221)
CD4 >200 cells/mm³ Within 8 weeks Lower immediate mortality risk; later initiation reduces IRIS risk SAPIT trial
CNS TB (any CD4) Delay 8 weeks CNS-IRIS is life-threatening; risk outweighs benefit of early ART in TB meningitis CAMELIA subgroup, WHO 2016

ATT Regimen (same as HIV-negative adults):

  • Intensive phase (2 months): Isoniazid (H) Rifampicin (R) Pyrazinamide (Z) Ethambutol (E) + Pyridoxine
  • Continuation phase (4 months): Isoniazid Rifampicin
  • Daily ATT is preferred over intermittent in HIV-TB (more efficacious; RNTCP/NTEP recommendation)
  • ART regimen for HIV-TB (first-line): TDF 300 mg + 3TC 300 mg + EFV 600 mg (single tablet at bedtime, under NACO/NTEP)
  • Co-trimoxazole preventive therapy (CPT) — for all HIV-TB patients regardless of CD4 count
  • Isoniazid Preventive Therapy (IPT) — for PLHIV without active TB after ruling out TB by 4S screening

Drug Interactions — Rifamycins & ART

Mechanism Rifampicin is a potent inducer of CYP3A4, CYP2B6, and P-glycoprotein → reduces plasma concentrations of PIs (by 35–92%) and NNRTIs (EFV by 22–26%, NVP by 31%). These sub-therapeutic levels risk HIV treatment failure and drug resistance emergence.
ART Class / Drug With Rifampicin With Rifabutin Recommendation
EFV (NNRTI) Compatible — AUC ↓22–26% only; EFV 600 mg/day adequate Use rifampicin — rifabutin not preferred with EFV EFV 600 mg + 2 NRTIs + rifampicin = preferred HIV-TB regimen
NVP (NNRTI) Avoid — AUC ↓31–55%; higher virological failure & hepatotoxicity Rifabutin preferred — no dose adjustment needed If EFV contraindicated, use NVP twice daily without lead-in; or switch to rifabutin
DTG (INSTI) Double dose — AUC ↓56%; increase DTG to 50 mg twice daily No dose change — rifabutin does not significantly affect DTG DTG 50 mg BD if rifampicin used; DTG 50 mg OD if rifabutin used
RAL (INSTI) Double dose — AUC ↓60%; increase RAL to 800 mg twice daily No dose change — rifabutin compatible RAL 800 mg BD with rifampicin; RAL 400 mg BD with rifabutin
EVG, BIC, CAB (INSTIs) Contraindicated — rifampicin reduces levels significantly; doubling dose insufficient Contraindicated — rifabutin also significantly reduces concentrations Avoid rifamycins with elvitegravir, bictegravir, cabotegravir
Boosted PIs (ATV/r, LPV/r, DRV/r) Contraindicated — PI levels ↓35–92%; high hepatotoxicity with super-boosting Rifabutin 150 mg/day — dose reduction to avoid toxicity (uveitis, neutropenia) Substitute rifampicin with rifabutin 150 mg/day when PI-based ART is used

Special Situations & IRIS

TB in PLHIV Already on ART (Table 21.2)

  • If on EFV-based ART → continue EFV + 2 NRTIs
  • If on NVP-based ART → substitute NVP with EFV
  • If on PI-based ART → substitute rifampicin with rifabutin 150 mg/day
  • If on RAL → increase RAL to 800 mg BD, or switch to rifabutin
  • If on DTG → increase DTG to 50 mg BD, or switch to rifabutin

Pregnant Women with HIV-TB

  • Avoid EFV in first trimester (teratogenicity concern — though WHO now allows post-1st trimester)
  • Preferred: TDF + 3TC + DTG (or RAL) backbone
  • Avoid streptomycin (ototoxic to fetus)
  • ART reduces MTCT (mother-to-child transmission)
  • Avoid didanosine + stavudine combination

MDR-TB + HIV

  • Mortality very high without appropriate treatment
  • Treatment for ≥18 months after culture conversion
  • Bedaquiline: ↓ levels with EFV → WHO recommends NVP substitution; use with caution with boosted PIs
  • Shared toxicities: peripheral neuropathy (linezolid + NRTIs), GI (fluoroquinolones + PIs)
  • Refer to Chapter 49 (Drug-resistant TB) for detailed regimens

IRIS in HIV-TB (Brief Summary)

  • Occurs in 7–43% of HIV-TB patients starting ART
  • Paradoxical IRIS: worsening of known TB after ART
  • Unmasking IRIS: occult TB revealed after ART start
  • Management: Continue ART; NSAIDs for mild; Prednisolone 1.5 mg/kg × 2 wks for moderate-severe
  • Prophylaxis: Prednisone 40 mg/day × 2 wks if CD4 <100 + ART within 30 days ATT (Meintjes, NEJM 2010)

Landmark Trials & Summary

CAMELIA Trial
NEJM 2011 | Cambodia
HIV-TB (n=661), median CD4 = 25 cells/mm³
ART at 2 weeks vs 8 weeks after ATT: 2-week arm reduced mortality by 38% (HR 0.62, p=0.006). IRIS more common in 2-week arm but manageable. Established early ART for CD4 <50.
SAPIT Trial
NEJM 2010 | South Africa
HIV-TB adults (n=642)
Integrated (early) ART significantly reduced AIDS/death vs. sequential (ART after TB completion). 64–95% reduction in mortality with ART during TB treatment.
STRIDE (ACTG 5221)
NEJM 2011 | Multi-country
HIV-TB (n=809)
Early ART (within 2 weeks) vs later (8–12 weeks): early ART reduced AIDS/death only in patients with CD4 <50. For CD4 >50, later ART had lower IRIS with equivalent outcomes.
Meintjes et al. (IRIS Trial)
NEJM 2010 | South Africa
TB-IRIS (n=110), randomised
Prednisolone 1.5 mg/kg × 2 weeks then 0.75 mg/kg × 2 weeks significantly reduced IRIS hospitalisation and need for invasive procedures. Established steroid regimen for TB-IRIS.
TEMPRANO / START
NEJM 2015 | Africa / Global
HIV-positive adults
Both trials independently showed that ART initiation irrespective of CD4 count (Treat All policy) reduces severe HIV morbidity including TB. Established "Treat All" WHO 2015 recommendation.
CPT Meta-analysis
Harries et al. Lancet 2010
HIV-TB, sub-Saharan Africa
Co-trimoxazole preventive therapy (CPT) halves the mortality risk in HIV-TB patients. Effective against PCP, Toxoplasma, diarrhoeal OIs. Recommended for ALL HIV-TB patients.
