01

Introduction & Historical Background

  • Arvid Wallgren (1889–1973) — Swedish paediatrician and tuberculosis pioneer; described the characteristic sequence of events following primary TB infection in children
  • His landmark work, published in the American Review of Tuberculosis (1948), described the timetable of events after primary infection based on meticulous clinical and tuberculin observations
  • Wallgren's contribution was instrumental in understanding primary TB as a dynamic, evolving disease with predictable temporal milestones
  • His observations were made in the pre-chemotherapy era — reflecting the natural history of untreated primary TB in children
  • The timetable remains clinically relevant today for understanding timing of complications, tuberculin conversion, and BCG immunity
  • Also known as "Wallgren's Timetable" or "Calendar of Events in Primary Tuberculosis"
Key Definition Wallgren's Table = A chronological timetable describing the sequence and timing of immunological, pathological, and clinical events following primary infection with Mycobacterium tuberculosis in children — from initial infection to potential late complications — typically spanning weeks to years
02

What is Wallgren's Table?

  • It is a timetable of events in primary tuberculosis — describing in chronological order what happens after a child inhales M. tuberculosis for the first time
  • Covers events from week 1 post-infection through years later — including immunological events, radiological changes, clinical manifestations, and potential complications
  • Key principle: Primary TB in children is not simply a pulmonary disease — it is a systemic disease with predictable sequential events driven by the interplay between bacterial multiplication and host immune response
  • Wallgren's timetable is NOT about the natural history of adult PTB — it specifically describes events in children (and uninfected adults) following their first-ever encounter with MTB (primary infection)
  • The timetable helps clinicians anticipate complications, time investigations, and counsel families about expected disease course

03

Primary TB Complex — Overview

  • Primary TB complex (Ghon's complex) = the triad formed after first infection: (1) Ghon's focus (lung parenchymal lesion) + (2) lymphangitis + (3) regional lymph node enlargement (hilar / paratracheal)
  • Site: Most commonly the subpleural region — well-ventilated areas — upper part of lower lobe or lower part of upper lobe (classically the "mid-zone" on CXR)
  • Ghon's focus: Named after Anton Ghon (Austrian pathologist, 1912) — the initial parenchymal lesion; typically 1–2 cm; may calcify → Ghon's calcified focus
  • Ranke complex: When both Ghon's calcified focus AND calcified hilar lymph nodes are visible on CXR → Ranke complex (named after Karl Ernst Ranke)
  • Unlike adult PTB — in primary TB, the lymph nodes are disproportionately enlarged relative to the parenchymal lesion (lymph node > parenchymal lesion in size)
  • Erythema nodosum and phlyctenular conjunctivitis — hypersensitivity manifestations that occur at the time of tuberculin conversion (3–8 weeks)
Ghon Focus vs Ranke Complex Ghon Focus = solitary calcified parenchymal scar (old primary infection) | Ghon Complex = Ghon focus + ipsilateral enlarged hilar/mediastinal LN + lymphangitis (active primary) | Ranke Complex = calcified Ghon focus + calcified hilar LN (healed/old primary infection seen on CXR)
04

Wallgren's Timetable — Complete Chronological Diagram

The following timeline traces events from the moment of first infection with M. tuberculosis through potential late complications over years:

Week 1–3
Implantation & Early Infection (Silent Phase)
Pre-allergic period · No symptoms · Tuberculin NEGATIVE
  • MTB inhaled → deposited in alveoli → ingested by alveolar macrophages
  • Bacilli multiply within macrophages (intracellular) → local spread to regional (hilar) lymph nodes via lymphatics
  • Ghon's focus begins forming — a small area of pneumonitis
  • NO systemic symptoms; tuberculin test NEGATIVE at this stage
  • Bacteraemia may occur at this very early stage — "occult" dissemination
Week 3–8
Tuberculin Conversion — Allergic Phase Begins
Hallmark event · TST / IGRA becomes positive
  • Tuberculin skin test (TST) becomes positive — usually 3–8 weeks (average 4–6 weeks) after infection; marks onset of cell-mediated immunity (CMI)
  • Granuloma formation — Langhans giant cells, epithelioid cells, lymphocytic cuffing around bacilli
  • Erythema nodosum — painful red nodules on shins; hypersensitivity reaction coinciding with tuberculin conversion
  • Phlyctenular conjunctivitis — nodular hypersensitivity lesion at limbus of cornea; concurrent with tuberculin conversion
  • Mild systemic symptoms may appear: low-grade fever, malaise, weight loss
  • CXR may show early hilar lymphadenopathy; parenchymal lesion small / not visible
Month 3–6
Peak Risk of Haematogenous Dissemination
Most dangerous period · Miliary TB · Meningitis risk
  • Highest risk period for haematogenous dissemination — especially in infants and young children (<5 years)
  • Miliary tuberculosis — occurs during this phase; diffuse bilateral micronodular shadows on CXR ("millet seeds"); most dangerous in infants
  • Tuberculous meningitis (TBM) — peak onset 3–6 months post-infection; carries highest mortality and morbidity of all TB manifestations; results from seeding of meninges during early bacteraemia
  • Pleural effusion (primary TB pleurisy) — may occur at this stage; exudative lymphocytic effusion
  • Primary progressive pneumonia — if immunity insufficient to contain Ghon focus → enlargement and progression
  • Hilar lymph nodes maximally enlarged; risk of bronchial compression → "collapse-consolidation" (epituberculosis)
Month 3–9
Pleural Effusion & Superficial Lymphadenitis
Hypersensitivity-mediated complications
  • Tuberculous pleuritis (pleural effusion) — characteristically occurs 3–9 months after primary infection; typically unilateral; exudative, lymphocytic, ADA elevated; rarely smear-positive; 5% of primary TB
  • Superficial lymphadenitis (scrofula) — cervical, axillary, inguinal nodes; collar-stud abscess formation; occurs 3–6 months post-infection
  • Erythema nodosum and phlyctenular conjunctivitis may persist or recur
  • Continued CXR changes: hilar LN enlargement, partial lobar collapse (segmental), areas of consolidation
Month 6–12
Healing or Early Complications
Resolution vs progression · Calcification begins
  • In majority — healing occurs: granulomas undergo central caseation → fibrosis → calcification → Ghon's calcified focus (visible on CXR after 6–12 months)
  • Hilar LN calcification begins; ultimately → Ranke complex if both Ghon focus and LN calcify
  • Bone and joint TB onset begins — spine (Pott's disease), hip, knee; most cases of bone TB present from 6 months to several years post-infection
  • Pericardial TB — can occur at 6–12 months; presents as pericardial effusion → risk of constrictive pericarditis
  • In infants and immunocompromised: disseminated TB can still occur at this stage
Year 1–3
Bone, Joint TB & Continued Healing
Late haematogenous seeding · Skeletal TB
  • Skeletal TB (Pott's disease of spine, hip joint, knee) — typically appears 1–3 years after primary infection; results from haematogenous seeding during the early bacteraemia
  • Spine involvement (Pott's disease) most common — lower thoracic / upper lumbar vertebrae; angular kyphosis; potential cord compression (Pott's paraplegia)
  • CXR: Ghon focus calcifying; hilar LN resolving or calcifying; parenchymal lesion clearing
  • Tuberculous dactylitis (spina ventosa) — bone TB in children involving phalanges; presents as painless soft-tissue swelling
  • TB of knee (white swelling / tumour albus) — cold abscess around knee joint
Year 3–5
Genitourinary Tuberculosis
Renal TB · Silent long-term sequela
  • Renal TB — classically appears 3–5 years after primary infection; one of the most delayed manifestations
  • Results from early haematogenous seeding of the renal cortex → dormant bacilli → reactivation years later
  • Presents as sterile pyuria, haematuria, recurrent UTI — "Sterile pyuria = TB until proven otherwise"
  • Can extend to ureter (strictures), bladder (contracted bladder), epididymis, seminal vesicles, fallopian tubes
  • Genital TB in females: commonest cause of TB-related infertility; endometrial TB → Asherman's syndrome
Years to Decades
Adult-type Post-primary TB (Reactivation)
Latent TB → endogenous reactivation
  • Endogenous reactivation — dormant bacilli in healed Ghon focus or apical seedings reactivate when immunity declines (immunosuppression, DM, HIV, malnutrition, ageing)
  • Produces post-primary (adult-type) PTB — upper lobe, cavitary disease; characteristic of reactivation TB
  • 5–10% lifetime risk of reactivation in HIV-negative individuals; annual risk 5–10% in HIV-positive
  • The Simon's focus — apical haematogenous seedlings from the early bacteraemia — are the typical site of reactivation in adults
  • This is why Wallgren's events are clinically important even in adults: the seeds of adult TB were sown during childhood primary infection

Mid-Phase Events (3–6 Months) — Complications

Most Dangerous Period The 3–6 month window after primary infection is the period of HIGHEST risk for life-threatening dissemination — miliary TB and tuberculous meningitis. Infants (<1 year) are at greatest risk. Untreated, mortality from TBM exceeds 25–30% even with treatment; sequelae in survivors are common.

09

Late Events (6–12 Months)

  • Pericardial TB: Haematogenous or direct extension from mediastinal LN → pericardial effusion; presents as muffled heart sounds, pulsus paradoxus, raised JVP; ECG — low voltage, ST changes; risk of constrictive pericarditis months to years later
  • TB of bones and joints: Onset 6 months to 3 years; Pott's disease (vertebral TB) most common — lower thoracic / upper lumbar vertebrae; cold abscess; angular kyphosis; Pott's paraplegia from cord compression
  • Tuberculous dactylitis (Spina Ventosa): TB of short tubular bones of hands/feet in children; painless fusiform swelling of digit; periosteal reaction; "blown-out" expansion of phalanx
  • Healing of primary complex: Ghon focus undergoes fibrosis and calcification → calcified nodule visible on CXR (Ghon's calcified focus); hilar LN also calcifies → Ranke complex; most children heal completely
  • TB of lymph nodes (Scrofula): Cervical chain most common; begins 3–6 months; may progress to collar-stud abscess (abscess pointing through cervical fascia); pathognomonic histology — caseating granulomas

Clinical Significance & Applications of Wallgren's Table

Diagnostic Applications

  • Helps clinician date the primary infection backwards from the presenting complication (e.g., TBM at 4 months → primary infection ~4 months ago)
  • Guides TST/IGRA interpretation — negative TST early (<3 weeks) does not exclude TB
  • Explains why renal TB presents years after the primary infection — haematogenous seeding is early, manifestation is delayed
  • Erythema nodosum + positive TST = recent primary infection even without pulmonary symptoms
  • Sterile pyuria in a patient with old calcified Ghon focus on CXR → strongly suggests renal TB

Preventive & Public Health Applications

  • BCG vaccination: Most effective if given before primary infection (at birth); protects against miliary TB and TBM (peak risk 3–6 months); does not prevent adult-type reactivation TB
  • Isoniazid Preventive Therapy (IPT): Must be given to contacts after excluding active TB; prevents progression from LTBI to active TB; window period of early bacteraemia is when IPT is most effective
  • Contact tracing: Household contacts especially children <5 years need TST at diagnosis and repeat at 8 weeks (window period) if initially negative
  • Guides TB preventive therapy timing in HIV-infected children — start IPT immediately after excluding active TB

Factors That Modify the Timetable

  • Age: Infants (<1 year) — fastest progression, highest risk of miliary/TBM; older children — slower; adults — different pattern (post-primary)
  • HIV co-infection: Accelerates all events; higher risk of dissemination at any stage; TST may be falsely negative
  • Malnutrition: Impaired CMI → faster progression; India — undernutrition major modifier
  • Measles: Causes temporary anergy (TST falsely negative); accelerates TB progression if concurrent
  • Immunosuppression: Steroids, anti-TNF, transplant → reactivation at any time

BCG & Wallgren's Events

  • BCG effective against: miliary TB (86% protection) · TBM (73–86% protection) · Primary complex (50–80%)
  • BCG does NOT reliably protect against: adult-type PTB · renal TB · reactivation disease
  • BCG converts TST to positive — must be interpreted carefully; IGRA distinguishes BCG from true infection
  • BCG-IRIS: BCG site swelling in HIV-infected infants on ART — follows Wallgren pattern
