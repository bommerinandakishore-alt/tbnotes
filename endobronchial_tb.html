Endobronchial Tuberculosis

Dhillon & Hanania (Textbook of TB)
Joshua & Roopa (Sharma's Textbook)
Chung & Lee, Chest 2000;117:385–92
▶ DEFINITION

Endobronchial tuberculosis (EBTB) is defined as tuberculous infection of the tracheobronchial tree with microbial and histopathological evidence, involving the trachea, main bronchi, segmental and subsegmental airways. First described by Richard Morton in 1689.

1

Epidemiology

  • Prevalence: Not exactly known; up to 40% of active TB patients at autopsy (range 3–72%)
  • Bronchoscopic studies: 10–20% of active TB patients; lower in recent studies due to timely chemotherapy
  • Non-endemic areas: Prevalence as low as 0.18%
  • Age: Predominantly 21–40 years; second peak in elderly (reactivation/reinfection)
  • Sex: Female preponderance — women suppress cough due to sociocultural/cosmetic reasons; female bronchi are structurally narrower
  • Indian data: 9.4% prevalence in 85 Indian children with active TB (bronchoscopic study); significantly higher prevalence of associated anthracofibrosis in Indian subcontinent immigrants vs other Asians (50% vs 3.7%, p<0.001)
2

Immunopathogenesis
  • Elevated IFN-γ and TGF-β levels in bronchial lavage fluid → pathogenesis and progression of EBTB
  • Lowered initial TGF-β + post-treatment TGF-β changes → implicated in bronchostenosis
  • Increased metalloproteinase-1 activity → increased risk of fibrosis and stenosis
3

Clinical Features

Symptoms

  • Barking cough — most common; unresponsive to antitussives
  • Dyspnoea (often with atelectasis)
  • Haemoptysis (occasional; rarely massive)
  • Chest pain (sternal/parasternal — ruptured node)
  • Wheeze / stridor — features of bronchostenosis
  • Fever (low grade → marked with progression)
  • Anorexia, weight loss, night sweats (may be minimal)
  • Bronchorrhoea (>500 mL/day) — rare

Signs

  • Diminished breath sounds
  • Localised fixed wheeze — low-pitched, constant, same area
  • Rhonchi
  • May disappear as airway progressively narrows

Mimics

  • Bronchial asthma
  • Foreign body aspiration (children)
  • Lung cancer (tumorous type)
  • Pneumonia / non-resolving pneumonia
Note: Symptoms may start years after diagnosis and treatment of pulmonary TB (due to post-stenotic disease). Acute presentations include respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation.
4

Diagnosis

A. Sputum Examination
  • AFB smear yield: low (15–20%)
  • Reason: mucus entrapment by proximal granulation tissue + lack of mucosal ulceration
  • Negative smear does NOT exclude EBTB
B. Chest Radiograph
  • Normal in 10–20% of patients
  • Patchy infiltrates, collapse (25–35%), consolidation (35–60%)
  • Hyperinflation, cavities, pleural effusion, miliary pattern, mediastinal lymphadenopathy
C. HRCT Chest
  • More sensitive than plain CT; endobronchial involvement detected in up to 95–97% with HRCT
  • Small airway involvement:
    • Centrilobular nodules (2–4 mm) — caseous material in terminal bronchioles
    • Tree-in-bud pattern — branching linear structures; stalk = last-order bronchus; tufts = alveolar ducts
    • Poorly defined nodules, bronchial wall thickening
  • Large/medium airways: circumferential luminal narrowing, peribronchial cuff, mediastinal nodes
  • CT cannot reliably differentiate endobronchial from submucosal or extrinsic lesion
  • Advantage: reveals airway beyond stenosis and length of stenosis — complements bronchoscopy

E. Bronchoscopy — Gold Standard
Bronchoscopy is the gold standard. Diagnosis can be missed in 10–20% of patients with normal CXR if bronchoscopy is not done. Airborne precautions are essential.

5

Complications

  • Bronchostenosis — most significant complication; develops in 50–90% despite effective therapy
  • Bronchiectasis — secondary to parenchymal fibrosis + central stenosis with distal dilatation
  • Atelectasis / lobar collapse; post-obstructive pneumonia; lung abscess
  • Obstructive emphysema; tension pneumothorax (one-way valve mechanism)
  • Tracheal stenosis → respiratory failure, difficult intubation, tracheostomy, death by suffocation
  • Pseudomembranous tracheobronchial aspergillosis on stenotic area (rare)
  • Expectoration of bronchial cartilages; bronchial fistula (rare)
  • Concomitant laryngeal TB (60%) and intestinal TB (82%) — in pre-chemotherapy era
Risk factors for bronchostenosis progression: Age >45 years; anti-TB treatment initiated >90 days after symptoms; tumorous/oedematous-hyperaemic type at bronchoscopy.

Corticosteroids
  • Used empirically with anti-TB drugs to prevent bronchostenosis — evidence remains controversial
  • Lee et al: suppress barking cough but may not alter disease course or prevent fibrostenosis
  • A randomised trial in adults failed to show benefit (RCT evidence negative)
  • May be useful in: children with lymph node TB causing bronchial obstruction; hypersensitivity reactions to anti-TB drugs
  • Current recommendation: No routine use of corticosteroids in EBTB
Surgery
  • Performed when disease is no longer active
  • Lobar / segmental stenosis → Lobectomy
  • Trachea / main-stem stenosis → Sleeve resection (bronchoplasty)
  • Extensive damage / obliterated mainstem → Pneumonectomy (last resort)
  • Modern surgical techniques: very low mortality; restenosis at anastomosis → repeat bronchoscopic dilatation may be needed

Differential Diagnosis

Endobronchial Mass

  • Bronchogenic carcinoma
  • Carcinoid tumour
  • Atypical mycobacteria (NTM)
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Actinomycosis
  • Papillomatosis

Airway Stenosis

  • Focal: Post-intubation, Crohn's disease, Behçet's syndrome
  • Diffuse: Wegener's granulomatosis, relapsing polychondritis, tracheobronchopathia osteochondroplastica, amyloidosis, rhinoscleroma
  • Anthracofibrosis — spares central airways, multiple lobar/segmental bronchi
Anthracofibrosis: Non-smoking elderly women, biomass fuel exposure; can co-exist with EBTB; anthracotic pigmentation on bronchoscopy → 60% have active TB (Chung et al)
