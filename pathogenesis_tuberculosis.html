Pathogenesis of Tuberculosis

10 Marks ~1000 Words Respiratory Medicine

Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb), an aerobic, non-spore-forming, acid-fast bacillus. The outcome of infection — whether the host contains or succumbs to the bacillus — depends critically on the interplay between mycobacterial virulence and host immunity. The pathogenesis involves a sequence of precisely orchestrated steps spanning innate and adaptive immune responses.

Key Principle: The development of anti-mycobacterial T-cell mediated immunity (specifically Th1 responses) is the single most important determinant of outcome in primary tuberculosis.

Step-by-Step Pathogenesis

1
Inhalation & Initial Deposition
  • Infection begins with inhalation of droplet nuclei (1–5 µm diameter) containing 1–5 viable bacilli
  • A single bout of cough produces ~3,000 droplet nuclei; a sneeze up to 1 million
  • Only bacilli that reach the terminal alveoli of the mid-lower lung (well-aerated zones) establish infection
  • Infectious dose: as few as 1–10 bacilli can initiate infection
2
Phagocytosis by Alveolar Macrophages
  • Bacilli are engulfed by alveolar macrophages (AMs) via pattern recognition receptors:
    • Mannose receptor (binds lipoarabinomannan on Mtb)
    • Complement receptor 3 (CR3 / CD11b)
    • Toll-like receptors (TLR-2, TLR-4, TLR-9)
    • DC-SIGN, Dectin-1
  • Phagocytosis forms a phagosome containing the bacilli
  • Non-opsonised bacilli are taken up less efficiently than opsonised ones
3
Evasion of Intracellular Killing — The Critical Step
  • In a naive, non-sensitised host, Mtb survives and replicates within the phagosome by:
    • Phagosomal maturation arrest: Sulfatides (cell wall glycolipids) block Ca²⁺ signalling → prevent recruitment of vesicular fusion proteins → phagosome–lysosome fusion fails
    • Resistance to acidification: Urease activity neutralises phagosomal pH; ammonia production inhibits acidification
    • Scavenging reactive oxygen species (ROS): Catalase-peroxidase enzyme (KatG) neutralises H₂O₂
    • Lipid bodies: Mycobacteria use host lipids as carbon source for slow intracellular replication
    • Inhibition of apoptosis: Mtb blocks caspase-dependent apoptosis, favouring necrosis which releases viable bacilli
  • Result: Unchecked bacterial proliferation within pulmonary alveolar macrophages and air spaces
4
Early Dissemination (First 3 Weeks)
  • Infected macrophages traffic to regional tracheobronchial lymph nodes via lymphatics
  • Bacteraemia (silent mycobacteraemia) seeds distant sites — apices of lungs, kidneys, bones, meninges
  • No visible lesions at distant sites in immunocompetent individuals at this stage
  • The primary parenchymal lesion + involved hilar lymph node = Ghon complex
5
Initiation of Adaptive Immunity (3–8 Weeks)
  • Infected macrophages/dendritic cells (DCs) migrate to lymph nodes and present mycobacterial antigens via MHC Class II to naïve T cells
  • DCs also present via MHC Class I → activation of CD8⁺ cytotoxic T cells
  • Antigen presentation drives expansion of Th1 CD4⁺ T lymphocytes
  • Sensitised T cells migrate back to site of infection and release:
    • γ-Interferon (γ-IFN): the master macrophage-activating cytokine
    • TNF-α: promotes macrophage activation, granuloma formation
    • IL-2: T-cell proliferation signal
    • IL-12: produced by macrophages → drives Th1 differentiation
    • Chemokines (CXCL10, CCL2): recruit additional macrophages and lymphocytes
6
Macrophage Activation & Granuloma Formation
  • γ-IFN activates macrophages → epithelioid cells (the morphological hallmark)
  • Activated macrophages now achieve phagosome–lysosome fusion and kill bacilli via:
    • Reactive oxygen intermediates (ROI)
    • Reactive nitrogen intermediates (RNI) — nitric oxide via iNOS
    • Lysosomal enzymes and defensins
    • Autophagy (LC3-associated phagocytosis)
  • Fused epithelioid cells → Langhans giant cells (nuclei arranged in horseshoe/peripheral pattern)
  • A rim of lymphocytes + fibroblasts surrounds the activated macrophage core = Granuloma
  • Tuberculin skin test (TST/Mantoux) becomes positive at this stage (~4–8 weeks post-exposure)
7
Caseous Necrosis — The Dual-Edged Response
  • Central granuloma undergoes caseous necrosis (cheese-like, eosinophilic, structureless debris on microscopy)
  • Mechanism: delayed-type hypersensitivity (DTH) reaction to tuberculoprotein → TNF-α mediated macrophage death
  • Within caseum: low O₂ tension, low pH, fatty acid accumulation → bacteriostatic environment
  • Outcome: Bacilli are contained but not eradicated — they remain dormant (latent TB)
  • In ~95% of immunocompetent individuals: fibrosis → calcification → Ranke complex (healed Ghon complex)

Factors Determining Disease Progression

Host Factors Favouring Progression

  • HIV/AIDS (CD4⁺ T-cell depletion — most potent risk factor; TB risk increases 20–30-fold)
  • Malnutrition (impairs lymphocyte function and macrophage activation)
  • Diabetes mellitus (impaired neutrophil and macrophage function)
  • Silicosis (quartz particles impair alveolar macrophage function)
  • Extremes of age — infancy and elderly (immature/waning immunity)
  • Immunosuppressive therapy — corticosteroids, TNF-α inhibitors (biologics), transplant drugs
  • Chronic kidney disease, alcoholism, tobacco smoking

Bacterial Virulence Factors

  • Cord factor (trehalose-6,6'-dimycolate): toxic to mitochondria; inhibits phagosome maturation; triggers granuloma formation
  • Lipoarabinomannan (LAM): inhibits macrophage activation by γ-IFN; scavenges reactive oxygen species
  • ESAT-6 / CFP-10 (ESX-1 secretion system): lyses phagosomal membrane → bacilli escape to cytoplasm
  • Antioxidant enzymes: KatG (catalase-peroxidase) neutralises H₂O₂
  • PE/PPE proteins: antigenic variation → immune evasion

Latent TB & Reactivation Mechanism

  • After primary infection, Mtb enters a metabolic dormancy state within granulomas
  • Within caseum: hypoxia + low pH + fatty acids → bacteria downregulate metabolic activity (DosR regulon)
  • Viable but non-replicating bacilli may persist for decades even in calcified lesions
  • Reactivation is triggered when:
    • CD4⁺ T-cell count falls below critical threshold (especially in HIV)
    • TNF-α blockade disrupts granuloma integrity
    • Intercurrent illness, malnutrition, ageing
  • Liquefaction of solid caseum (via macrophage proteases) → cavity formation → massive bacillary load → high transmission potential
Important: Anti-TNF biologics (infliximab, adalimumab) used in rheumatoid arthritis and IBD are a potent risk for reactivation TB — they disrupt granuloma architecture. Mandatory TB screening (IGRA/TST) is required before initiating these agents.

Pathogenesis in the Immunocompromised (HIV/AIDS)

  • CD4⁺ T-cell depletion → loss of γ-IFN → macrophage activation fails → granuloma formation impaired
  • Histopathology shows poorly formed or absent granulomas — replaced by aggregates of macrophages containing numerous AFB
  • Non-reactive TB (disseminated, necrotic, no cellular reaction) in late-stage AIDS
  • Extrapulmonary TB occurs in up to 70% of HIV-TB co-infected patients (cf. ~20–25% in immunocompetent)
  • False-negative TST and sputum smears are common ("anergy")
  • IRIS (Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome) after ART initiation may unmask or worsen TB

Conclusion: The pathogenesis of TB is a dynamic battle between mycobacterial immune evasion strategies and host granulomatous immunity. The granuloma — while serving to contain infection — also enables bacterial persistence, making TB a lifelong adversary. Understanding these mechanisms underlies the rationale for both anti-TB chemotherapy (targeting metabolically active and dormant bacilli) and immunotherapy-based approaches to prevention and treatment.
